    Ionisation Energy

    Definition

    First Ionisation energy is the energy required to remove 1 mole of electrons from 1 mole of gaseous atoms to form 1 mole of unipositively charged gaseous ions.

    \[ X_{(g)} \rightarrow X^{+}_{(g)} + e^- \]

    Definition

    The nth Ionisation energy is the energy required to remove 1 mole of electrons from 1 mole of gaseous ions with a charge of (n - 1)+ to form 1 mole of gaseous ions with charge of n+.

    \[ X^{(n - 1)+}_{(g)} \rightarrow X^{n+}_{(g)} + e^- \]

    Factors affecting ionisation energy

    • Number of quantum shells. The larger the number of quantum shells in an atom, the further the electron is from the nucleus and hence experiences weaker nuclear attraction and is easier to remove, thus the lower the ionisation energy.
    • Effective Nuclear Charge, \(Z_{eff}\). The higher the effective nuclear charge, the stronger the attrative forces between teh nucleus and the electrons to be removed, hence the greater the ionisation energy.

    Trend in the Successive Ionisation Energies of the Same Element

    \[ IE_1 < IE_2 < IE_3 < IE_4 < ... \]
    • As electrons are removed from an atom, the nuclear charge remains constant as number of protons remains constant.
    • However, the shielding effect is reduced as the number of electrons decreases.
    • Hence, effective nuclear charge increases and the electrostatic attraction between the nucleus and the outmost electron becomes stronger
    • Thus, more energy is needed to overcome the stronger attraction and remove the electron.
    • IE increases.

    Application of Successive Ionisation Energies

    Suppose we have the successive IE of an unknown atom, we can identify the group of this atom from the successive IE.

    • The first big increase is from \(2^{nd}\) to \(3^{rd}\) ionisation energies.
    • This is because the third electron is from an inner quantum shell.
    • Thus there are two electrons in the outermost value shell.
    • Therefore, this atom is from group 2.

    Important

    • Generally, a large increase implies removal of an inner quantum shell electron.
    • A small increase implies removal of an inner subshell electron in the same quantum shell.

    Trend in the First Ionisation Energies Down the Group

    Down the group

    • The nuclear charge increases as proton number increases
    • The shielding effective increases as number of inner electrons increases
    • Thus, effective nuclear charge remains relatively constant.
    • As the number of quantum shell increases, the electrostatic forces of attraction between the nucleus and the outermost electron is weaker.
    • Therefore, less energy is required to overcome the weaker attraction
    • As a result, the first ionisation energy decreases.

    Trend in the First Ionisation Energies Across Period 2

    Across Period 2

    • The number of quantum shells remains constant
    • Nuclear charge increases as the number of protons increases
    • Shielding effect remains relatively constant as additional electron is added to the outermost quantum shell
    • Thus, effective nuclear charge increases and the electrostatic force of attraction between nucleus and outermost eletron is stronger
    • More energy is required to overcome the stronger attraction.
    • Therefore, the first ionisation energy increases across period 2 and 3.

    There are two dip in first IE

    • From Group 2 to Group 3
    • From Group 15 to Group 16

    Dip from Group 2 to Group 3

    Electronic Configuration of Group 2 atom: \(ns^2\)

    Electronic Configuration of Group 3 atom: \(ns^2 \, np^1\)

    • The first IE of the group 2 atom involves the removal of s electron while the first IE of the group 3 atom involves the removal of p electron.
    • Since the p electron is further away from the nucleus than the s electron.
    • There is weaker attraction between the nucleus and the outermost electron.
    • Hence less energy is required to remove the p electron, resulting in a lower ionisation energy.

    Dip from Group 15 to Group 16

    Electronic Configuration of Group 15 atom: \(ns^2 \, np^3\)

    Electronic Configuration of Group 16 atom: \(ns^2 \, np^4\)

    • All p electrons in the Group 15 atom are unpaired, two of p electrons in Group 16 are paired.
    • There is some inter-electronic repulsion between the paired electrons in the p subshell of the Group 16 atom.
    • Thus less energy is required to remove one of the pair electron from the Group 16 atom.

    Trend in the First Ionisation Energies Across Transition Elements

    • The first IE involves removal of a 4s electron.
    • The nuclear charge increase due to increasing proton number across transition elements.
    • The shielding effect also increases as additional electron is added to the inner d subshell, thereby nullifying the influence of each additional proton.
    • Thus effective nuclear charge remains almost constant.
    • Since the number of quantum shells is constant, the energy required to remove the outermost electron remains largely invariant.
